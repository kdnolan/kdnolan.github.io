Think, Puzzle, Explore: Year 6 students put their AI thinking on the wall

Facilitated by Kieran Nolan and Katie Gunn  |  A Visible Thinking routine from Project Zero, Harvard Graduate School of Education

Visible Thinking Think Puzzle Explore Artificial Intelligence Project Zero EdTech

Who created AI? How does it think so fast? What can't it do better than a human? And could it ever become conscious? On Monday morning, Year 6 students at Boneo Primary School raised every one of these questions themselves, before a single lesson on Artificial Intelligence had been taught.

That was exactly the point. The session was a Think, Puzzle, Explore (TPE) routine, one of the Visible Thinking routines developed by Project Zero at the Harvard Graduate School of Education. Rather than opening a new topic with instruction, TPE opens with the students' own minds: what do you think you know about this topic? What puzzles you about it? And how could you explore it further?

Run at the very start of Year 6's AI unit, the routine gave facilitators Kieran Nolan and Katie Gunn something no pre-test could: an honest, unfiltered map of what 39 students actually believe, wonder and misunderstand about the technology reshaping their world.

A thinking wall, live and anonymous

What made this session different was how the thinking became visible. Students submitted their responses through an anonymous Google Form, and a custom-built live response wall pulled them straight onto the SPARK Centre's new interactive touchscreen, appearing in three colour-coded columns within moments of being written.

The anonymity mattered. With no names attached, students who rarely volunteer in class discussion put their genuine thinking on the wall alongside everyone else's. And the live display mattered too: watching the columns fill in real time created a sense of shared momentum, and gave the whole room a common object to think with. Later, the touchscreen let the group scroll back through the wall together, moving responses around and drawing connections by hand.

Confident, but puzzled

Alongside their responses, students rated how well they feel they understand AI right now, from 1 to 5. The results say a lot about growing up in 2026: the mean rating was 3.5, and nearly six in ten students placed themselves at 4 or 5. These are children who use, see and hear about AI every day, and they know it.

But the written responses complicate that confidence in the most productive way. Students could readily name what AI does, yet their puzzles clustered around the most foundational questions of all: where AI came from, how it works, and what its limits are. That gap between confidence and depth is precisely what TPE is built to expose, and it now gives the unit ahead its shape.

What the wall revealed

In the Think column, students described AI first and foremost as a helper: something for schoolwork, planning and fixing things. But there were flashes of a more critical eye. One student wrote:

“Although AI is extremely intelligent, it still hasn't mastered the arts of acting like a human. You can still tell by little hints if something is made by AI.” Year 6 student, Think column

6B's wall told the same story in its own voice, with an added note of ambivalence. One student summed up a tension the whole room seemed to feel:

“I think it is pretty interesting because who knows what else it does, and it's pretty useful too, but it's still horrible for people.” Year 6 student, Think column

And where a real mental model was missing, imagination filled the gap. One student theorised that “Megamind lives inside a computer and makes AI work”. Playful, yes, but also a genuine data point about how children explain a technology nobody has ever shown them the inside of.

The Puzzle column was dominated by one question in different forms: who made this? “Who created AI?”, “who made AI and why?” and “how long did it take to code and make AI?” appeared across both classes, a reminder that students meet AI as a finished product with no visible history. Close behind came questions of mechanism and speed:

“How does AI think of so many questions and create so many things at once so quickly?” Year 6 student, Puzzle column

And at the edges, some strikingly sophisticated wonderings: what is AI not better than humans at, and how could an AI come to have self-consciousness. These are questions that working researchers and ethicists are still arguing about today.

The Explore column showed students already thinking like researchers about sources. They named AI tools themselves, search engines, and, tellingly, the humans around them: their SPARK teachers “who know a lot about coding and how things work.” The instinct to triangulate between digital tools and trusted people is one of the strongest foundations a class can bring to an AI unit.

From puzzles to plans

The session closed where the best sessions do: in conversation. Gathered around the touchscreen, the group worked through the Puzzle column together, clustering similar questions and talking through how each could actually be investigated.

The origins question became a plan to trace AI's history: when the term was coined, who the pioneers were, and how today's AI companies fit into that story. The speed-and-mechanism puzzles became an idea to ask AI tools to explain themselves, then test those explanations against what teachers and reliable sources say. The limits question turned into a proposed experiment: find tasks where humans still beat AI, and practise spotting the “little hints” that give machine-made work away.

And the consciousness question was given the honest treatment it deserves: some puzzles have no settled answer, even among experts, and that makes them more worth exploring, not less.

Why it matters

By the end of the hour, the Year 6 cohort had produced its own curriculum map. The four anchor inquiries for the AI unit (origins, mechanism, limits, and consciousness) came not from a textbook but from the students' own puzzles, captured anonymously, displayed live, and discussed openly. When the unit ends, the same self-rating question will be asked again, and the wall will be waiting for the comparison.

The routine itself is simple. Three questions, one form, one screen. But as Monday showed, when students can see the whole room thinking, the thinking gets better.
